# AM32-ESC
Custom AM32-compatible high-current BLDC Electronic Speed Controller (ESC) featuring an STM32G030 MCU, three-phase MOSFET inverter, sensorless Back-EMF detection, current sensing, integrated BEC, and optimized PCB layout.
---

# AM32 Based High Current Electronic Speed Controller (ESC)

This project is a custom-designed three-phase Electronic Speed Controller (ESC) built around the **AM32 open-source firmware**. The objective of this design is to provide a compact, high-current motor controller capable of driving sensorless BLDC motors used in robotics, UAVs, autonomous underwater vehicles (AUVs/ROVs), and other embedded motion control applications.

The hardware has been designed specifically to provide all the necessary peripherals required by the AM32 firmware, including a three-phase power stage, gate driving circuitry, Back-EMF sensing network, battery voltage measurement, current sensing, onboard power supplies, and debugging interfaces. Each subsystem has been carefully designed to ensure reliable operation under high switching frequencies while maintaining accurate feedback for the firmware's internal control algorithms.

# Main Controller

At the heart of the ESC is the **STM32G030** microcontroller from STMicroelectronics, based on the ARM Cortex-M0+ architecture. The STM32G0 family was selected due to its high-performance timers, fast Analog-to-Digital Converters (ADCs), low interrupt latency, and efficient processing capabilities, making it well suited for real-time motor control applications.

The microcontroller is responsible for executing the **AM32 firmware**, which performs multiple real-time tasks simultaneously. These include generating complementary PWM signals for the MOSFET gate driver, reading the motor phase voltages for Back-EMF detection, monitoring battery voltage and motor current, executing sensorless commutation algorithms, and implementing the firmware's closed-loop speed control. Since motor commutation timing directly depends on the measured feedback signals, accurate acquisition of these analog inputs is essential for stable and efficient motor operation.

The board also exposes an **SWD/JTAG programming interface**, allowing firmware flashing, debugging, breakpoint analysis, and firmware development using STM32 development tools. A UART interface is additionally provided for communication and debugging purposes.

# Three-Phase Power Stage

The power stage consists of six **Texas Instruments CSD19531Q5A NexFET™ N-Channel MOSFETs** arranged in a conventional three-phase half-bridge topology. Each motor phase is driven by one high-side and one low-side MOSFET, allowing bidirectional current flow through the motor windings during commutation.

The CSD19531Q5A was selected because of its excellent switching characteristics and very low **RDS(on)** of approximately **3.1 mΩ (typical)**, which significantly reduces conduction losses during operation. The device also offers low gate charge, allowing faster switching transitions and reduced switching losses at higher PWM frequencies.

According to the Texas Instruments datasheet, the MOSFET is capable of handling continuous drain currents exceeding **100 A under specified thermal conditions**. In practice, however, the continuous current capability of the complete ESC depends on several additional factors including PCB copper thickness, thermal vias, heat sinking, airflow, switching frequency, ambient temperature, and overall thermal design. Consequently, the final current rating of the ESC is determined by the complete hardware implementation rather than the MOSFET specification alone.

The combination of low conduction losses and fast switching performance enables higher overall efficiency while reducing heat generation during continuous operation.

# Three-Phase Gate Driver

Driving high-power MOSFETs directly from a microcontroller is neither practical nor electrically safe, as the GPIO pins cannot supply the gate voltage or current required for fast switching. To solve this, the ESC uses the **FD6288Q three-phase gate driver**, which acts as the interface between the STM32 and the MOSFET power stage.

The FD6288Q integrates three high-side gate drivers and three low-side gate drivers into a single package, allowing all six MOSFETs to be controlled using the PWM outputs generated by the microcontroller. Internally, the driver incorporates high-current gate drive stages capable of rapidly charging and discharging the MOSFET gate capacitances, resulting in faster switching transitions and reduced switching losses.

The gate driver also includes important protection features such as **Under Voltage Lockout (UVLO)**, which prevents the MOSFETs from operating when the supply voltage is insufficient for proper gate enhancement. This helps avoid excessive heating caused by partially turned-on MOSFETs. Additional internal circuitry also minimizes the possibility of shoot-through conditions by ensuring proper gate drive timing between the high-side and low-side switches.

# Bootstrap Circuit

Since the ESC employs N-channel MOSFETs on both the high-side and low-side of each half bridge, the gate voltage of the high-side MOSFET must always be driven several volts above the corresponding motor phase voltage. Achieving this requires a floating gate supply.

Each motor phase therefore includes an independent **bootstrap circuit**, consisting of a high-speed bootstrap diode and a bootstrap capacitor connected to the FD6288Q gate driver.

During the interval when the low-side MOSFET is conducting, the bootstrap capacitor charges from the driver's supply voltage. When the high-side MOSFET is commanded to turn on, the stored charge inside the capacitor provides a floating supply that allows the gate driver to raise the gate voltage above the motor phase potential. Without this bootstrap arrangement, the high-side MOSFET could never be fully enhanced, severely limiting the operation of the inverter.

Bootstrap gate driving is widely used in modern motor controllers because it provides an efficient and compact solution without requiring isolated power supplies for each high-side driver.

# Back-EMF Sensing Network

Sensorless BLDC control relies on accurately detecting the **Back Electromotive Force (Back-EMF)** generated by the motor as it rotates. To facilitate this, each motor phase is connected to a precision resistor divider network that scales the phase voltages into the safe operating range of the STM32 ADC inputs.

The sampled voltages are continuously processed by the AM32 firmware to detect the zero-crossing points of the Back-EMF waveform. These zero crossings provide an estimate of the rotor position, allowing the firmware to determine the correct instant to switch the active motor phases during six-step commutation.

Because the commutation timing is entirely dependent on these measurements, careful design of the resistor divider network is essential to minimize noise while maintaining accurate voltage scaling across the complete operating voltage range.

# Current Measurement

Motor current is measured using the **Texas Instruments INA199A1DCKR**, a precision current-sense amplifier designed for high-side current measurement applications.

A low-value shunt resistor placed in the power path develops a small voltage proportional to the motor current. The INA199 amplifies this differential voltage while rejecting the high common-mode voltage present on the supply rail, producing an analog output suitable for direct measurement by the STM32 ADC.

Current feedback serves multiple purposes within the firmware. It enables real-time current monitoring, over-current protection, stall detection, and provides additional information about motor loading. These measurements improve the reliability and safety of the controller while protecting both the motor and power electronics from excessive current conditions.

# Battery Voltage Monitoring

The ESC continuously monitors the battery voltage using a resistor divider connected to one of the STM32 ADC channels. The divider scales the input voltage to within the ADC's allowable operating range while maintaining minimal loading on the battery.

The measured battery voltage is used by the AM32 firmware to implement under-voltage protection, estimate the available supply voltage, and provide telemetry information where supported. Continuous monitoring of the supply voltage also allows the firmware to compensate for voltage variations that naturally occur as the battery discharges.

# Power Supply Architecture

The ESC incorporates a dedicated onboard power supply architecture to generate the stable voltage rails required by both the power electronics and digital control circuitry.

The primary regulated supply is generated by the **JW5026 synchronous buck converter**, which steps down the battery input voltage to a regulated **5 V rail**. This supply powers the FD6288Q gate driver and other onboard peripherals while providing high conversion efficiency over a wide input voltage range.

To provide a cleaner supply for the digital electronics, the regulated 5 V output is further processed by an **AP2210K-3.3TRG1 Low Dropout (LDO) regulator**, producing a stable **3.3 V rail** for the STM32 microcontroller and associated analog circuitry. Using an LDO after the switching regulator significantly reduces switching ripple and electrical noise, improving ADC accuracy and overall system stability.

Appropriate decoupling capacitors are distributed throughout the PCB to minimize supply transients, reduce high-frequency noise, and ensure stable operation during rapid switching events.

# Overall Operation

During operation, the STM32 executes the AM32 firmware, continuously acquiring motor phase voltages, battery voltage, and current measurements through its ADC channels. Based on the detected Back-EMF zero crossings and the desired throttle command, the firmware computes the appropriate commutation sequence and generates high-frequency PWM signals.

These PWM signals are supplied to the FD6288Q gate driver, which rapidly drives the six CSD19531Q5A MOSFETs forming the three-phase inverter. The inverter then applies the required voltage vectors across the motor windings, producing the torque necessary to rotate the BLDC motor. This closed feedback loop repeats thousands of times per second, allowing precise speed control, efficient power conversion, and reliable sensorless motor operation across a wide operating range.

# PCB Layout and Design Considerations

The PCB layout was designed with a strong emphasis on electrical performance, thermal management, and signal integrity. Since the ESC operates by switching high currents at high frequencies, careful component placement and routing are essential for achieving efficient operation while minimizing electromagnetic interference (EMI) and switching losses.

# Component Placement

The PCB has been partitioned into three primary functional sections: the power stage, control circuitry, and power supply. The six MOSFETs, gate driver, and bootstrap components are grouped together to form a compact switching stage, minimizing the distance between these critical components. The STM32G030 microcontroller and analog sensing circuitry are positioned away from the switching nodes to reduce electrical noise coupling, while the buck converter and LDO occupy a dedicated section of the board to provide clean and stable power to the digital circuitry.

# Power Routing

High-current paths between the battery input, MOSFET bridge, and motor phase outputs have been implemented using wide copper pours to reduce resistive losses and improve current-carrying capability. The routing has been kept as short and direct as possible, minimizing parasitic resistance and inductance while improving overall power conversion efficiency.

Particular attention was given to reducing the area enclosed by the high di/dt switching loops, helping to minimize voltage overshoot, ringing, and EMI during fast switching transitions.

# Signal Integrity

Sensitive analog signals, including the Back-EMF sensing network, battery voltage divider, and current sensing amplifier, are routed separately from the power stage to minimize coupling of switching noise into the STM32 ADC inputs. Local decoupling capacitors are placed close to each integrated circuit to provide low-impedance paths for transient currents and maintain stable supply voltages during switching events.

# Thermal Management

Since the majority of power dissipation occurs within the MOSFET bridge, the PCB incorporates large copper pours around the power stage to spread heat over a greater surface area. Multiple thermal vias beneath the MOSFET thermal pads provide an efficient heat transfer path to the opposite PCB layer, reducing junction temperature and improving long-term reliability under sustained high-current operation.
